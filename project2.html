Predicting the exchange coupling in copper complexes

GitHub-project

Summary and Overview

The magnetic characteristics of dinuclear copper(II) complexes have been extensively explored using both experimental and computational methods.[1][2][3][4][5][6][7][8][9] Various factors influence the magnetic interaction of paramagnetic centres in close vicinity. Notably, structural changes and exchange interactions are closely related. For example, subtle changes in the metal centre's distances and angles can cause significant alterations in the sign and magnitude of J.

Hatfield and Hodgson's seminal work[9] established a link between the Cu-OH-Cu (θ) angle and the exchange coupling constant J in certain bis-(μ)-hydroxo copper(II) dimers [Cu(OH)2L2]2+. Current knowledge confirms that altering geometrical parameters around the metal centre can modify a complex's magnetic properties.

Quantum chemistry studies based on DFT methods from Ruiz and co-workers supported the results observed by Hatfield and Hodgson and also revealed that the out-of-plane angle (α) of bridging ligands, such as hydroxo, is equally crucial as the α angle.[2][3] A pronounced α angle often corresponds to a reduced α angle, leading to ferromagnetic coupling. This α angle, especially in bis-μ-hydroxo complexes, is influenced by hydrogen bonding with adjacent counterions. However, no formal correlation between θ, α and J has been established using quantum chemical approaches.

Implementation and Results

Discrepancies between computed and experimental J values seem more important when considering μ-hydroxo bridging ligands, showing that a suitable description of the magnetic properties for these systems is only possible if α is appropriately included. This latter observation is a major concern because the X-ray diffraction technique does not adequately identify the positions of the hydrogen atoms. It becomes clear that to achieve a more precise description of the magnetic properties of metal complexes featuring a bis-μ-hydroxo bridging ligand, the position of the hydroxo hydrogen must be properly considered. Therefore, a new correlation connecting the α and θ angles with the Jcalc was established using complex 1 as a reference (Figure 1).

Model complexes featuring seven θ angles from 95˚ to 101˚ were designed, and the α angle was scanned from 0˚ to 60˚. The computed J values were all extracted from the previous BS calculations using model complexes, the TPSSh functional and def2-TZVP basis set, and a three-dimension correlation (3D) was obtained (Equation I, Figure 2) (see GitHub link for the code implementation):

\[ J_{\text{calc}} = 7.50 \times 10^3 - 9.78 \times 10^{-1} \theta - 7.49 \times 10^1 \alpha + 1.81 \times 10^{-1} \theta^2 - 6.39 \times 10^{-3} \theta^2 \alpha - 3.70 \times 10^{-4} \theta^2 \alpha^2 - 4.22 \alpha^2 - 7.95 \times 10^{-2} \alpha^2 \theta + 1.46 \theta \alpha \]

As previously discussed, the exact position of the out-of-plane (α) angle cannot be precisely known due to the intrinsic limitations of X-ray diffraction techniques hindering the routinary resolution of the hydrogen atom. Therefore, the strategy is to use the experimental values of both the Cu-OH-Cu angle (θ) and the exchange coupling constant (Jexp) to estimate the out-of-plane (α) angle using equation. After extracting the value of α from the 3D equation, we use it to change the out-of-plane angle within the crystal structures of a series of selected bis-μ-hydroxo dinuclear copper(II) complexes obtained from the literature. Then, the computed J using the fitted 3D α value (J3D) is extracted using BS-DFT computations. Finally, we calculated J using the X-ray-determined value of α (JXray) and compared it to the value obtained using the 3D α angle (J3D) to evaluate the performance of the 3D correlation. To assess the performance of the 3D correlation to predict the experimental exchange coupling constant, the absolute difference (AD), the absolute percentage deviations (APD), the mean absolute difference (MAD) and the mean absolute percentage (MADP) deviation were used (Table 1).

    The determination of the AD shows that using the 3D correlated α value significantly improves the exchange constant's calculated values, reproducing the magnetic interaction's nature and also moving towards a quantitative prediction of J. For instance, the ICISUJ complex shows an ADX-ray of 666 cm-1, with a value of JX-ray = 758 cm-1 that is quite far off from the experimental one of Jexp = 92 cm-1. However, if the α angle is now adjusted with the one extracted from the 3D correlation, the AD3D decreases drastically to a value of 18 cm-1. This shows the significant improvement of the J prediction by taking into account not only the θ angle, as is usually the case with the Hatfield and Hodgson correlation, but also the α angle. This is also reflected in the mean absolute differences since the high value of MADX-ray of 132 cm-1 suggests a significant difference between the experimental and calculated results, while a relatively low MAD3D of 11 cm-1 is obtained by using the 3D correlation. The same rationale applies when looking at the resulting ADP values, which are systematically lower when the α angle is extracted from the 3D correlation for all complexes listed in Table 1 and Figure 3.

    Conclusion

    It becomes clear that the out-of-plane angle has to be accounted for a more precise prediction of the J values in bis-μ-hydroxo dinuclear copper(II) complexes. Combining the two main geometrical parameters that influence the sign and value of J, we constructed a three-dimension correlation between α, θ and J. This correlation significantly improves the prediction of the J values when α is extracted and adequately corrected in the crystal structure.

    Perspectives

    Magnetic properties are the consequence of the individual contribution of the metal ions, ligands, and counterions, and the final geometry resulting from the ligand-metal coordination. Thus, a deep understanding of the micro- and macroscopic factors that govern the magnetic properties of polynuclear metal complexes is crucial. Furthermore, it is the first step towards a better understanding of the relationship between structure and magnetism, opening the door for better rationalizing reaction mechanisms. Therefore, realizing how the structure shapes the magnetic interaction in small systems is vital in predicting the magnetic behaviour of larger and more complicated frameworks. Moving forward, we need to dig deeper into the magnetic mechanisms using quantum computing. Also, there's a growing interest in magneto-structural correlations beyond just dinuclear copper complexes because of their potential application in magnetism and bioinspired design of novel catalyst.
