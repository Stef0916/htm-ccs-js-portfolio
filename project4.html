Oxygen evolution reaction by self-assembled Nickel cubanes

View Publication

Summary and Overview

Transforming solar energy into chemical energy is one of the most important processes sustaining life on earth. In the case of photosynthesis, it involves the four-electron oxidation of dioxygen into water driven by solar light.[1] Organisms that are able to convert light into fuel rely on a membrane protein called Photosystem II (PSII). The active site of PSII, where the water oxidation occurs, is known as the oxygen-evolution complex (OEC), comprised of a Mn4O5Ca cluster (Figure 14a).[2] Hence, catalytic water oxidation, as part of water splitting strategies, could provide abundant fuels from renewable sources, which is much needed judging from the current energetic crisis. Unfortunately, catalytic water oxidation is a global challenge representing the bottleneck for water splitting, and much remains to be done. Taking inspiration from the OEC topology where PSII can accumulate four electrons within a tetrametallic-oxo cluster, one can imagine a bioinspired model featuring metal-oxo subunits, leading to “cubane”-type M4O4 complexes (Figure 1b).

In this work, we report the study of the electronic structure, magnetic properties, and reactivity towards water oxidation of two tetranuclear Ni4O4 cubane-like complexes 3 and 4 (for further details on experimental View Publication) (Scheme 1).

Full geometry optimizations of the two complexes using their X-ray crystal structures as starting points were performed in the gas phase. Standard deviations were calculated and found in the range of DFT precision, i.e., 0.035–0.043 Å and 1.5–1.6° for bond lengths and angles of 3 and 3, respectively. Single point calculations using the optimized geometries provided electronic structures of the two complexes. From Mulliken spin population analysis (Figure 2), we observe that around 92% of the spin density is distributed on the Ni(II) metal centres, as expected for the case of a tetranuclear complex having four high spin (HS, S=1) Ni(II) ions.

Complexes 3 and 4 have four Ni(II) centres assumed to be in the high spin configuration due to their octahedral environments, thus each site features two unpaired electrons. Magnetic interactions between these paramagnetic centres are therefore expected and can be described by the exchange coupling constant, J. BS-DFT was used to extract the six expected exchange coupling constants J12, J13, J14, J23, J24 and J34 based on the eight possible spin configurations of the system S = 4, Ms = 2 and Ms = 0 (3) (Figure 3).

For the methodology followed to extract the J values go to project 6. The following J values were calculated: J12 = 3.3 cm-1 and 2.0 cm-1, J13 = 3.3 cm-1 and 2.9, J14 = 1.7 cm-1 and 2.9 -1, J23 = 1.6 cm-1 and 4.0 cm-1, J24 = 1.7 cm-1 and 2.9 cm-1, J34 = 3.4 cm-1 and 1.6 cm-1 for complexes 3 and 4, respectively. We thus observe that all the calculated coupling constants between the nickel sites are positive, which demonstrates the presence of ferromagnetic interactions between the paramagnetic centres, in agreement with previous experimental observations.[3][4][5][6]

Water oxidation reaction (example with complex 3):

  • Ligand exchange: substitution of MeOH by H2O was considered, and a relaxed surface scan was utilized in order to calculate the feasibility of this ligand exchange. A Gibbs free energy difference of -7.1 kcal mol-1 was obtained, supporting the feasibility of the ligand exchange process.
  • Cl dissociation: After the first ligand exchange, the dissociation of one Cl anion from one Ni centre and the formation of a bond between the metal centre and an external water molecule was investigated. As a result, Gibbs free energy differences of 2.8 kcal mol-1 was obtained, supporting the feasibility of the ligand exchange process.
  • NiII to NiIII oxidation: oxidation processes of one nickel ion per water-substituted cubane providing Ni(III) species within the [(μ3LnO)4Ni4Cl3(H2O)5]2+ complex was investigated by DFT calculations and lead to computed redox potentials of 1.16 V versus RHE. These two values are reasonably close to the experimentally determined onset potentials for OER by complexes 3, supporting the monoelectronic nature of the observed electrochemical events.
  • Deprotonation of water molecule: to prove that the reaction mechanism goes through an I2M mechanism, the next expected step in the reaction mechanism is the exergonic deprotonation of a water molecule coordinated to the formally Ni(III) centre. This step, which would result in the formation of a Ni(III)–OH moiety, is supported by our DFT computations with calculated free energy differences of -31.5 kcal mol-1 for complex 3 using HPO42- as base at pH 7.
  • EPR parameters: the spectral features were simulated considering a single paramagnetic species, leading to three principal g values at g1 = 2.045, g2 = 2.053, g3 = 2.061 with a nitrogen hyperfine coupling constant of 14AN = 25 MHz. The g-values are consistent with a S = 1/2 NiIII-centred speciessup>[7][8] (Figure 5) and a good agreement was found when comparing these data with the DFT-computed EPR parameters for complex 3 in its oxidized form, g1 = 2.031, g2 = 2.038, g3 = 2.075.

Conclusion

The robust nickel cubanes 3 and 4 can be easily prepared in good yields from readily available starting materials under ambient conditions. These tetranuclear complexes are active for electrocatalytic water oxidation at neutral pH as proven by combining experimental and theoretical approaches. The BS-DFT approach was successfully employed in the study of the magnetic properties of the Ni-cubanes, as well as to elucidate their ground-state electronic structure. Using the HDvV Hamiltonian within the BS-DFT framework, we provided the computed magnetic sublevels that allow the assignment of both ground and first excited states. Furthermore, theoretical reactivity studies confirmed the feasibility of the oxygen evolution carried out by both Ni-cubanes.

Perspectives

Extension of this self-assembly strategy for preparing other water oxidation catalysts with benzimidazole-based ligands and earth-abundant metals (Co and Fe) is currently underway using both experimental and theoretical tools.
