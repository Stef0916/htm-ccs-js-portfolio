O-atom transfer mediated by bioinspired copper complexes

View Publication

Summary and Overview

Copper is found in many enzymatic systems and is considered a natural and biological atom which is readily available, as opposed to heavier transition metals. This bioavailability makes it safer and more enviromentally firendly metal to use in catalysis and medicinal chemistry. For instance, the Lytic Polysaccharide Monooxygenase (LPMO) is capable to break recalcitrant polysaccharide, such as cellulose and chitin. Given the world's demand in energy and the societal impulse to find new sustainable alternatives, there is great interest in studying these enzymes because of their potential use in the production of biofuels from second-generation biomass.[1][2][3] Nonetheless, further improvements will depend, most likely, on better understanding the enzymes' interactions with the substrates and its mechanisms of actions. Copper-containing enzymes carry out diverse activities, including electron transfer, dioxygen binding, activation and reduction, and denitrification processes. In the active sites, the copper centre can be found in various coordination modes such as mononuclear, dinuclear, trinuclear, and tetranuclear clusters.[4]

The reductive activation of dioxygen is a crucial process in biology and represents a major challenge for the industry.[5] Of particular importance are those copper enzymes that can bind dioxygen either to transport it, reduce it into hydrogen peroxide or water, or incorporate one of its oxygen atoms into a substrate.[6] Copper can bind dioxygen in various ways leading to mononuclear and dinuclear copper-oxygen adducts, such as CuII-superoxide, CuII-hydroperoxide or high-valent copper-oxo species, as well as binuclear CuII-peroxide or CuIII-oxide adducts (Scheme 1).

Especially, Cu2O2 species have caught great attention since several copper-containing enzymes activate dioxygen to oxidize organic substrates using dinuclear copper intermediates. A few years ago, the group of Réglier designed tridentate RPy2-type ligands (RPy2 = (N,N-bis-[2-2(-pyridyl)ethyl] alkylamine)) bearing either an activated (IndanylPy2 = IndPy2; L1) or non-activated (cyclopentylPy2 = cPtPy2; L2) substrate (Scheme 2).[7][8]

Starting from mononuclear CuI complexes (1-L1 and 1-L2) in CH2Cl2, different results were obtained upon exposure to dioxygen (Scheme 2). Reaction with the activated ligand L1 yielded the stereoselective hydroxylated cis-2-amino-1-indol product in 50% yield. However, no hydroxylation was observed when the non-activated L2 was used under the same reaction conditions, resulting in a quantitative recovery of the initial ligand (Scheme 3). Hypotesis regarding the reaction mechanism points towards the reaction of Cu(I) precursor with dioxygen leads to the formation of the a dinculear peroxide species (P) which is in equilibrium with the oxo species (O).

In the present work, we investigated the ability of such dinuclear intermediates to perform C-H bond activation of L1 and L2 using a computational approach. Different mechanistic steps were confronted with the experimental observations to derive a structure-property relationship between the ligand effect and the reactivity. Therefore, to construct and optimise the dinuclear species, we considered model complexes holding one entire ligand (L1 or L2) on one copper centre and one truncated ligand L3 on the other (Scheme 4).

Implementation and Results

  • Cu2O2 adducts formation
  • Exchange coupling constant in CuII µ-η2:η2-peroxide copper
  • Optimization of transition states
  • Peroxo-oxo isomerism
  • Regio and Stereo-selectivity
  • Hydroxylation reaction
  • Natural Bond Orbital analysis

The computational investigation of dinuclear copper species represents a major challenge due to the intrinsic nature of the dinuclear Cu2/O2 adducts. While the Cu2III(µ-oxo) core (O) displays two formal CuIII ions bound to two bridging O2- anions, the Cu2II(µ-η2:η2-peroxo) isomer (P) features two Cu(II) ions coordinated to a O22- ligand. The O intermediate is formally a closed-shell system, and the P isomer is in an open-shell arrangement. Therefore, the isomerisation equilibrium between P and O represents a bottleneck for computational methods since the singlet ground spin state cannot be properly described by a single determinant-methods, while multiconfigurational approaches remain rather costly for medium-to-big sized systems.[9][10][11][12][13] In the present study, we followed the recommendation of Gupta et al. where they performed benchmark for the P/O isomerisation equilibrium and C-H activation was carried out using CCSD(T) calculations as the reference method.[14]

The peroxo-oxo isomerization is the first step of the reaction mechanism based on the extensive literature regarding this kind of dinuclear system. This reaction involves the formation of a peroxide species that is in equilibrium with an oxo species, which in turn is the responsible of the C-H activation (Figure 1 - O/P isomerization). Our results show that the oxo isomer is thermodynamically accessible in both cases (for the activated and non-activated ligand). In addition, they highlight that the two complexes, while having different overall reactivity, display a similar behaviour for the isomerisation reaction. Thus, these findings imply the limited influence of the ligand on the P/O isomerisation.

Interestingly, when looking at the overall computed mechanism for the L1-based model (Figure 1), it can be observed that the H-abstraction step has a relatively low activation barrier compared to the P/O isomerisation step (1.9 vs 8.4 kcal.mol-1, respectively). This suggests that P/O isomerisation accounts for the rate-determining step (r.d.s) of the reaction. These findings agree with conclusions from kinetic studies by the group of Itoh with similar ligand systems.[15][16] Our results are thus consistent with the difference in reactivity observed when using ligands L1 and L2, respectively, and provide a solid basis to rationalise the previous experimental observations.

Conclusion

In the present study we elucidated the regio- and stereoselectivity of benzylic C-H bond hydroxylation (L1) and the lack of C-H activation of the cyclopentyl substituent (L1). We analyzed the dinuclear side-on peroxide species (P) Cu2II(µ-η2:η2-O2) and the key intermediate bis-(μ-oxo) isomer (O) Cu2III(µ-O)2, believed to be central to the C-H activation process. For the indanyl ligand (L1), the P/O isomerization resulted to be the rate-determining step, leading to a subsequent low energy barrier for the H-abstraction by the O intermediate. In contrast, H-abstraction from the cyclopentyl substituent (L2) demands a higher activation energy than for L1, which aligns with the observed lack of hydroxylation. Hence, our findings explain the limited reactivity of O species with tridentate RPy2 ligands towards potent C-H bond activation.

Perspectives

When Cu(II) precursors were placed in the presence of a reducing agent (benzoin) and dioxygen, non-activated C-H bond hydroxylation was achieved.[7][8] This raises the question of the nature of the intermediates formed under these conditions. Further work is currently in progress to address this question.
