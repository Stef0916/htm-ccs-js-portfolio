Magnetic properties of tetranuclear Ni-square grid

Results to be published

Summary and Overview

In the last decade, the design and synthesis of 3D polynuclear metal complexes that result from self-assembly through linking molecules in a non-covalent fashion have caught the attention of many researchers.[1][2][3][4][5] Organic molecules obtained by the condensation of ketones (or aldehydes) and carbohydrazide or thiocarbohydrazide have previously led to the self-assembly of polynuclear metal complexes of various topologies such as square and rectangular grids.[6][7][8]

Ni(II) metal ions can adopt different geometries and topologies depending on the ligand and the reaction conditions, allowing a very flexible design of new polynuclear Ni(II) complexes. In this work, we report the study of the magnetic properties of a tetranuclear square grid Ni(II) resulting from the self-assembly between a thiocarbohydrazide based-ligand and nickel nitrate complex 1 (Figure 1).

Implementation and Results

Calculation of exchange coupling constant:

To better understand the magnetic behaviour of complex 1, theoretical calculations based on DFT were performed using the H-optimized crystal structure. The complex consists of four magnetically interacting Ni(II) centres and can be reasonably well described by the broken symmetry (BS)[9][10] approach, which will allow to determine the sign and magnitude of the six coupling constants J12, J13, J14, J23, J24 and J34.

For these calculations, we first assumed that locally all spins on each metal centre are aligned in parallel (local high spin configurations). Then, a separate solution for each of the possible spin configurations of the cluster is calculated with a positive or negative sign of the local spin on each centre. In principle, the number of spin configurations expected for a complex with n magnetic centres is 2n-1. This leads to a total number of eight different spin configurations whose energies can be calculated for complex 1. Assuming a non-symmetric structure, one can therefore generate a high spin (HS) configuration S = 4, four broken symmetry (BS) solutions Ms = 2 and three BS Ms = 0 solutions (Figure 2).

The energy of each spin configuration can be written as a linear combination of the local spin of each magnetic centre and the individual exchange coupling constant resulting in a series of eight equations as follows:

High-spin state (S = 4)

Eαααα = -2J12(1×1) -2J13(1×1) -2J14(1×1) -2J23(1×1) -2J24(1×1) -2J34(1×1)

Single flip (Ms = 2)

Eαααβ = -2J12(1×1) -2J13(1×1) -2J14(1×(-1)) -2J23(1×1) -2J24(1×(-1)) -2J34(1×(-1))

Eααβα = -2J12(1×1) -2J13(1×(-1)) -2J14(1×1) -2J23(1×(-1)) -2J24(1×1) -2J34((-1)×1)

Eαβαα = -2J12(1×(-1)) -2J13(1×1) -2J14(1×1) -2J23((-1)×1) -2J24((-1)×1) -2J34(1×1)

Eβααα = -2J12((-1)×1) -2J13((-1)×1) -2J14((-1)×1) -2J23(1×1) -2J24(1×1) -2J34(1×1)

Double flip (Ms = 0)

Eαββα = -2J12(1×(-1)) -2J13(1×(-1)) -2J14(1×1) -2J23((-1)×(-1)) -2J24((-1)×1) -2J34(1×(-1))

Eαβαβ = -2J12(1×(-1)) -2J13(1×1) -2J14(1×(-1)) -2J23((-1)×1) -2J24((-1)×(-1)) -2J34(1×(-1))

Eααββ = -2J12(1×1) -2J13(1×(-1)) -2J14(1×(-1)) -2J23(1×(-1)) -2J24(1×(-1)) -2J34((-1)×(-1))

Using the ORCA quantum chemistry software and applying the previous equations, the system of linear equations were solved using the singular value decomposition (SVD) approach. The following J values were calculated: J12 = -92 cm-1, J23 = -144 cm-1, J34 = -90 cm-1, J14 = -155 cm-1, and J13 = J24 = -1 cm-1. We thus observe that the coupling constants between the diagonal nickels are much lower than those of the adjacent nickels, which is expected given the distance between them. More interestingly, all the coupling constants calculated are negative, which demonstrates the presence of antiferromagnetic interactions between the nickel centres. From the calculated coupling constants, the energies and nature of the ground state of the system and its excited states can be simulated. This is achieved by direct diagonalization of the HDvV Hamiltonian and by employing the orca_eca utility of ORCA program. The magnetic sublevel spectrum of the tetranuclear species is composed of 19 states: 3 singlets (S = 0), 6 triplets (S = 1), 6 quintets (S = 2), 3 seven-fold (S =3) and 1 nine-fold (S = 4) of energy. These data allow us to conclude the nature of the ground state of the system, which is a singlet, S = 0, separated by 247 cm-1 from the first excited state, which is S = 1.

In addition to the above analysis, we also reconstructed the theoretical magnetic susceptibility curve (blue curve, Figure 2) using the calculated exchange coupling constants and using the Easy-spin program. These data were superimposed with those from the experimental measurements (red curve, Figure 2). This highlights the very good agreement between theory and experiment. It also confirms the validity of the results obtained from the calculations concerning both the fundamental spin state (singlet, S =0) and the nature of the magnetic interactions (antiferromagnetic).

Conclusion

The self-assembly between a thiocarbohydrazide based-ligand and nickel nitrate yielded the tetranuclear Ni(II) complex 1. The X-ray crystal structure revealed a boat-like geometry within the Ni-S-Ni plane featuring a square grid geometry with four Ni(II) ions bridged by four sulphur atoms. The magnetic interaction between Ni(II) was proven to be antiferromagnetic with weak a J between diagonal Ni(II) (J13 and J24) as expected due to the long Ni-Ni distance. Furthermore, and based on previous magneto-structural correlations on similar systems, antiferromagnetic coupling was expected due to the large values of the Ni-S-Ni angles (between 151.2 and 158.4°), in accordance with experimental results.[11][12][13][14] BS-DFT calculations were in complete agreement with experimental results showing an antiferromagnetic coupling for all the six theoretical J’s, featuring a weak coupling for diagonal exchange coupling constants (J13 and J24). Finally, we were able to reconstruct the theoretical magnetic susceptibility curve, which is in excellent agreement with the experimental one.
