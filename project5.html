Bioinsipired catechol-alloxazine Ni-trinuclear complex

View Publication

Summary and Overview

In the present study, the coordination of nickel with an alloxazine-based bioinspired ligand was studied and yielded a trinuclear Ni(II) complex denoted 1, featuring three redox-active ligands comprised of a catechol and a flavin-related alloxazine moiety (Scheme 1). Here, we report an exhaustive analysis of this unique trinuclear complex's electronic structure and magnetic properties using quantum chemical approaches. Further information regarding the experimental details of this study can be found in the publication

Several methods were used in the study of this trinuclear nickel complex to elucidate its electronic structure. Among these methods, we employed Density Functional Theory (DFT) specifically to assist in interpreting the experimental results, which include EPR, SQUID, mass spectrometry, and X-ray analysis. This helped shed light on the most probable electronic states of the system. For further details on all the listed calculations and computational aspects, View Publication).

Implementation and Results

Electronic structure:

Catechols and alloxazines both stabilize radicals when bound to metals. For alloxazines, protonation can occur on the pyrazine ring nitrogens (Scheme 1). Thus, it's plausible the complex could be partly or fully protonated, allowing magnetic coupling between the Ni(II) ions and the ligand's organic radicals (SQ.) (Scheme 2). In fact, X-ray analysis of the solid-state structure reveals Ni(II) centres influenced by varying electronic environments from interactions with water and DMF molecules. This structure also hints at partial protonation in the alloxazine sections based on C-N distances.

To differentiate these possibilities and clarify the ligand's protonation and radical states in the trinuclear complex 1, DFT calculations were done. According to Scheme 2, three configurations were examined with three high-spin Ni(II) ions each: one with three protons and radicals, another with two of each, and the last with just one proton and either one or three radicals. All models underwent geometry optimizations, and their free energies were determined.

Based on energetic considerations and in agreement with SQUID analysis (see section SQUID measurement), the models with one proton and either one or three organic radicals are too high in energy compared to the other configurations and will not be considered any longer. The model with three protons and three organic radicals appears as the most stable one, but a fully protonated state of the ligand is not supported by experimental data obtained from X-ray crystallography and High-Resolution Mass Spectroscopy (HRMS). Therefore, the hypothesis that matches the closest to the experimental results would be the presence of 3 HS Ni(II) centres (S = 1) and 2 organic radicals delocalized within the catechol-alloxazine ligand (SQ., S=1/2), which is achieved by a partial protonation of the two flavin nitrogen (Figure 1).

Modelling of magnetic interactions in model systems:

According to our previous results, triangle 1 features SQ●-radicals delocalized in the vicinity of the Ni(II) ions, which allows to describe the triangle as a system of three high spin Ni(II) ions (S = 1) and two SQ● radical (S = ½). This generates a magnetic interaction between the paramagnetic centres that can be described by the exchange coupling constant J using the HDvV Hamiltonian[1][2] within the BS-DFT framework, which will provide the theoretical magnetic sublevel spectrum to assign both ground and first excited states.

The calculated exchange coupling constant for the Ni-SQ. pair, JNi-SQ, for a model with 1 Ni-center and 1 radical, was found to be 131 cm-1, which indicates a strong ferromagnetic interaction between the Ni(II) metal centre and the SQ.-radical, leading to a quartet (S = 3/2) ground spin state with a doublet (S = 1/2) excited state lying at 393 cm-1. These results are in excellent agreement with the experimental SQUID measurements, which proposed a strong ferromagnetic coupling within the Ni-SQ. moiety.

The magnetic orbitals for model A obtained from the BS-DFT calculations are reported in Figure 2. These orbitals are orthogonal, which is also consistent with the calculated overlap integral featuring a small magnitude of S = 0.009. These data thus agree with a strong ferromagnetic interaction within this model.

In order to calculate the interaction between two Ni-SQ● moieties, a model with 2 Ni-centers and 2 radicals was used to perform the BS-DFT calculation. The computed J(Ni-SQ)-(Ni-SQ) value for model B is 15 cm-1, which indicates a weak ferromagnetic interaction between the two Ni(II) moieties which leads to a 7-fold (S = 3) ground spin state with a quintet excited state (S =2) lying at 90 cm-1>. This is in fair agreement with the experimental results, where a weak interaction between the two moieties was expected.

Conclusion

Upon combining a catechol-alloxazine ligand with a nickel ion, a unique trinuclear species was obtained and characterized in the solid state. Its electronic structure has been studied by a multidisciplinary approach combining several techniques. The presence of protons and ligand-based radicals was established, as well as the presence of high spin Ni(II) centres strongly ferromagnetically coupled with semiquinone radicals, SQ.. Using the BS[3][4] approach within the DFT framework, we were able to predict and confirm both the electronic structure and the magnetic interactions within this peculiar system. Using two model systems, our BS-DFT computations provided to two different exchange coupling constants, JNi-SQ = 131 cm-1 and J(Ni-SQ)-(Ni-SQ) = 15 cm-1 describing two types of interactions: (i) within the pair composed of the metal centre and the semiquinone radical, Ni-SQ, and (ii) between two pairs composed of the metal centres and the semiquinone radicals, (Ni-SQ)–(Ni-SQ). These results are in fair agreement with the experimental data obtained from SQUID measurements and further support that complex 1 can be best described as a triangle with two protons on the ligand and three high-spin Ni(II) ions combined to two ligand-based radicals.
